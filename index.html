SINGAPORE, STRAWBERRIES AND THE GLOBAL SCALE

Singapore relies heavily on imported food, including strawberries, which are sourced from various countries. If there is a supply chain disruption or shortage of strawberries in one country, Singapore can usually find alternative sources of supply to meet the demand for the fruit. However, various factors can affect the availability and prices of strawberries in Singapore, including supply and demand, weather conditions, and trade policies. Like any other country, the cost and availability of strawberries in Singapore can be influenced by global and regional market forces.

For example, seasonal variations in supply and demand can impact the availability and prices of strawberries in Singapore. As strawberries are a seasonal crop, their availability and prices can vary depending on the time of year and the country of origin. During winter months, when strawberry production is limited in some countries, the demand for imported strawberries in Singapore may increase, leading to higher prices or shortages of the fruit. Conversely, during peak strawberry season in countries like Australia, when there is an oversupply of the fruit, the prices of imported strawberries in Singapore may decrease.

Additionally, trade policies, such as import tariffs or restrictions on certain fruits, can affect the cost and availability of strawberries in Singapore. If a major supplier of strawberries to Singapore imposes an import tariff on the fruit, the prices of imported strawberries in Singapore may increase, making them less affordable for consumers. Similarly, if a country imposes restrictions on the import of strawberries due to safety or phytosanitary concerns, the availability of strawberries in Singapore may decrease.

A real-life example of how market forces can affect the availability and prices of strawberries in Singapore is the shortage of strawberries in December 2020. This was due to a combination of factors, including seasonal weather conditions, increased demand in other markets, and supply chain disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic. As a result, the prices of strawberries in Singapore increased significantly, and consumers reported difficulties in finding fresh strawberries in supermarkets and online grocery platforms. To address the shortage, retailers in Singapore turned to alternative sources of strawberries, including countries like Australia and the United States, which were experiencing a peak in their strawberry season. However, the higher cost of importing from these countries led to higher prices for consumers. The shortage of strawberries in Singapore also highlighted the country's dependence on imported food and the vulnerability of its food supply chain to global market forces.

SO WHAT IS SINGAPORE DOING?

Singapore is not known for producing its own strawberries due to its tropical climate and limited land availability. However, the city-state is actively promoting sustainable agriculture and food security through various initiatives, including vertical farming, aquaponics, and rooftop gardening. These methods enable the cultivation of vegetables and herbs in urban areas, reducing Singapore's reliance on imported food. Its efforts in promoting sustainable agriculture and food security are significant steps towards achieving a more self-sufficient and sustainable food system.

Strawberries grown in a vertical farm in Singapore have made their debut, marking a milestone in the country's efforts to promote local food production. The strawberries are grown in a high-tech, indoor vertical farm using hydroponic technology, which allows plants to grow without soil. The farm, run by a local company called Sustenir Agriculture, is capable of producing up to 150kg of strawberries per month. The initiative is part of Singapore's larger push to promote sustainable agriculture and food security through innovative and technology-driven methods of food production.
